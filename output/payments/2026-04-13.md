**GoCardless reaches first profitable quarter as payment volumes double**
*The open banking payments firm's milestone suggests that bank-to-bank payment processing at scale can sustain a viable business model.*

GoCardless reported its first quarter of positive adjusted EBITDA in FY25, alongside 22% revenue growth and a doubling of payment volumes to £79.2bn. The company attributed much of the volume increase to its earlier acquisition of open banking firm Nordigen, which broadened its pay-by-bank capabilities across European markets.

The profitability milestone matters beyond GoCardless itself. Open banking payment initiation has faced persistent questions about unit economics – whether account-to-account payments can generate enough margin at scale to justify the infrastructure investment. A company processing over $130bn annually reaching profitability is the clearest evidence yet that the model can work, at least for firms with sufficient volume and geographic reach.

The results come alongside a restructuring that will cut around 90 roles, and amid reports that Mollie is pursuing an acquisition of GoCardless. If confirmed, that deal would create one of Europe's largest independent payment processors with deep open banking capabilities – a consolidation play that reflects where the market is heading.

**Wise to move primary listing from London to the US**
*The cross-border payments firm's departure from the LSE is the latest blow to London's standing as a listing venue for high-growth fintechs.*

Wise confirmed plans to transfer its primary listing to the United States next month, following strong Q4 growth in volumes, customers and income. The company listed in London in 2021 via a direct listing and has been among the most prominent fintechs on the exchange.

The move reflects a now-familiar calculation: US markets offer deeper liquidity, higher valuations and a larger pool of growth-oriented investors. For European payments specifically, it narrows the set of publicly traded, Europe-headquartered firms available to investors on local exchanges. Wise joins a pattern of European fintechs either listing in the US from the outset or migrating there after initially choosing a domestic venue.

For London, the loss is reputational as much as financial. The UK government and FCA have spent the past two years trying to make the LSE more attractive to technology companies. Wise's exit undercuts that effort directly and will intensify pressure for further reforms to listing rules and market structure.

**Federal Reserve consults on cross-border capability for FedNow**
*Extending the US instant payment system internationally would create a new corridor competitor and accelerate pressure on correspondent banking economics.*

The Federal Reserve has opened a consultation on enabling FedNow participants to make cross-border transfers through the real-time payments network. The proposal would connect FedNow's domestic instant payment rails to international counterparts, though specific corridor details and interoperability mechanisms have not yet been published.

For European payments firms, this is worth watching closely. The EU's instant payments regulation is already reshaping domestic euro payments; a cross-border FedNow capability would add a new dimension to transatlantic payment flows. Corridor-specific instant payment links – such as those being developed between IPS systems in Asia and the Nordics – have so far been bilateral arrangements. A US Federal Reserve-backed cross-border layer would be qualitatively different in scale and ambition.

The timing is notable. SWIFT's transaction management platform is being positioned as the interoperability layer between domestic instant payment systems globally. A FedNow cross-border extension could either complement or compete with that approach, depending on how it is architected.

*Editorial note: A FedNow cross-border expansion has direct strategic implications for European instant payment operators, cross-border fintechs and correspondent banks.*

**EBA moves to harmonise SEPA reporting across member states**
*Standardising how national regulators report under the SEPA regulation should reduce fragmentation that has complicated compliance for cross-border payment providers.*

The European Banking Authority published a decision setting out harmonised reporting requirements for national competent authorities under the SEPA Regulation. The move aims to eliminate inconsistencies in how different member states collect and report SEPA-related data, which have created uneven supervisory outcomes and additional compliance burden for firms operating across multiple jurisdictions.

This is an infrastructure-level change rather than a headline-grabbing one, but it matters for payments processors and banks running pan-European SEPA operations. Divergent national reporting requirements have been a persistent friction point, particularly for newer market entrants and payment institutions that lack the regulatory affairs capacity of large banks. Harmonisation should reduce that overhead and improve the EBA's ability to monitor the single payments market consistently.

The decision also fits within the broader regulatory push – alongside PSR, instant payments mandates and PSD3 preparation – to make the single euro payments area function more like an actual single market rather than a patchwork of nationally supervised regimes.

*Editorial note: A practical regulatory development that directly affects operational compliance for any firm processing SEPA payments across borders.*

**UK consumer warnings highlight growing pains for pay-by-bank adoption**
*As open banking payments reach 30 million monthly transactions in the UK, consumer protection gaps are drawing mainstream media attention.*

Money Saving Expert issued a public warning about the risks of using pay-by-bank (account-to-account) payments on major e-commerce platforms including Amazon and eBay. The concern centres on the lack of chargeback and Section 75 protections that card payments provide – meaning consumers who pay by bank transfer have significantly less recourse if something goes wrong with an order.

The warning comes as UK open banking payment volumes have passed 30 million successful transactions per month, according to the Open Banking industry body. That growth makes the consumer protection gap increasingly material rather than theoretical. While the payments industry has been focused on adoption metrics and conversion rates, the absence of a standardised buyer protection framework for A2A payments remains the most significant barrier to broader e-commerce use.

This is a policy problem as much as a market one. The UK's Smart Data legislation and the work of the future open banking entity are expected to address protections, but nothing concrete is yet in place. Until it is, mainstream consumer media flagging the risks will act as a drag on adoption – and a reminder that payment method competition is fought on trust as much as cost.

*Editorial note: Consumer protection for open banking payments is a live policy question in the UK and EU; mainstream media coverage turning negative makes it more urgent.*
