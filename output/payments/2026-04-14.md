**FCA publishes open finance roadmap with regulatory framework due by end of 2027**

*The UK regulator has laid out a four-year plan to extend data-sharing obligations beyond payments accounts to investments, pensions and insurance — the clearest signal yet of how open finance will be governed in the UK.*

The Financial Conduct Authority today published its vision for open finance, setting out a phased approach that targets a regulatory framework by end of 2027 and full implementation by 2030. The scope is deliberately broad: investment platforms, pension providers and insurers are all pulled into the net, meaning firms in those sectors now face a concrete — if not imminent — compliance horizon.

The practical detail matters more than the ambition. The FCA is encouraging firms to begin offering open finance services voluntarily ahead of regulation, a tactic that worked unevenly with open banking. The regulator cites existing examples — Mastercard and Saxo Bank's open banking-funded investment accounts in Denmark — as evidence that the plumbing can work across asset classes. The mortgage sector is called out specifically as a near-term beneficiary, with data-sharing expected to streamline affordability checks.

For payments and open banking professionals, this is the document that turns "open finance" from a policy aspiration into an operational planning concern. The 2027 regulatory framework date is the one to watch. Firms building open banking infrastructure should now be designing with extensibility in mind — the FCA has made clear that payments data access is the floor, not the ceiling.

*Editorial note: This is the most consequential UK regulatory development in open finance to date, setting concrete timelines that will shape product and compliance planning across financial services.*

**GoCardless reaches profitability as it prepares commercial VRP launch**

*The UK-based account-to-account payments firm posted its first profitable quarter and confirmed commercial variable recurring payments will launch later this year — a test of whether recurring pay-by-bank can scale beyond sweeping use cases.*

GoCardless reported 22% revenue growth in FY25 and its first quarter of profitability, a milestone for a firm that has been investing heavily in open banking payments alongside its core direct debit business. The company confirmed it will launch commercial variable recurring payments later this year, branding the product as "recurring Pay by Bank".

The profitability headline matters less than the VRP timing. Commercial VRP — where consumers authorise ongoing variable payments to merchants — remains one of the most-discussed but least-deployed open banking capabilities. GoCardless is well-positioned to test the proposition given its existing merchant base and direct debit expertise, but mass-market adoption will depend on consumer trust, bank participation and the still-evolving regulatory stance on non-sweeping VRP.

Separately, FinTech Magazine reported on Mollie's acquisition of GoCardless, framing the deal as a consolidation play that combines Mollie's European merchant reach with GoCardless's A2A and recurring payments capabilities. If the VRP launch coincides with integration into Mollie's merchant network, the distribution opportunity shifts meaningfully.

*Editorial note: GoCardless reaching profitability while preparing to launch commercial VRP marks a concrete step in the open banking payments story — and the Mollie acquisition context adds a consolidation angle worth tracking.*

**Wise to move primary listing from London to the US**

*The cross-border payments firm's decision to shift its primary listing to New York is the latest in a pattern of European fintechs choosing US capital markets — and a pointed signal about London's diminishing pull for high-growth payments companies.*

Wise announced it will transfer its primary listing from the London Stock Exchange to the US next month, following strong Q4 growth in volumes, customers and income. The company has maintained a London listing since its 2021 direct listing on the LSE.

The move is not a surprise — Wise has been expanding its US customer base and revenue share — but it is significant as a data point. Wise joins a growing list of European-origin fintechs that have either listed in the US or shifted listings there, citing deeper liquidity, higher valuations and proximity to their largest investor base. For London, which has been trying to attract and retain technology listings through reforms to its listing regime, losing one of its highest-profile fintech names is a setback.

For the European payments sector more broadly, the question is whether this reflects a structural disadvantage in European capital markets or simply the gravitational pull of the US for companies at Wise's scale. Either way, it changes the competitive dynamics: a US-listed Wise will face different investor expectations and potentially have greater access to capital for acquisitions.

*Editorial note: Wise's re-listing is a significant strategic shift with implications for both the cross-border payments competitive landscape and the broader debate about European capital markets' ability to retain payments firms.*

**PSD3 and PSR implications mapped for non-EU financial centres**

*A Mondaq analysis of how PSD3 and the Payment Services Regulation will affect Switzerland highlights the extraterritorial reach of EU payments regulation — relevant for any non-EU market with deep ties to the single market.*

A legal briefing published by Mondaq examines the practical implications of PSD3 and the PSR for the Swiss fintech industry, noting that the reforms carry "considerable practical significance" for a financial centre closely integrated with the EU. The analysis covers the PSR's approach to open banking friction — requiring banks to provide dedicated interfaces that meet performance standards — and the broader shift toward a more prescriptive regulatory framework.

The Swiss angle is instructive beyond Switzerland. The PSR's requirement for compensation when interfaces fail, its expanded scope for account access and the open banking 2.0 provisions will create compliance obligations that extend to any non-EU firm serving EU customers or connecting to EU payment infrastructure. For UK-based firms navigating both the FCA's open finance roadmap and EU regulatory developments, the gap between the two regimes is becoming a practical product design consideration.

*Editorial note: With the FCA's open finance roadmap published today, the contrast and interaction between UK and EU approaches to open banking regulation is increasingly operational — this analysis usefully maps one non-EU perspective on the PSR.*

**AI vulnerability discovery in open banking APIs raises security urgency**

*Anthropic's latest AI model has demonstrated the ability to autonomously discover exploitable flaws in banking APIs, compressing what previously took security teams weeks into hours and raising questions about the resilience of open banking infrastructure.*

PYMNTS reports that Anthropic's Claude Mythos Preview model has begun autonomously detecting vulnerabilities in open banking API implementations, identifying flaws that would typically require specialist human expertise and weeks of analysis. The piece argues that AI-driven security threats are a more immediate risk to open banking infrastructure than the much-discussed quantum computing threat.

The concern is straightforward: open banking's security model relies on API interfaces that are complex, rapidly evolving and maintained by institutions with varying levels of security maturity. If frontier AI models can identify exploitable weaknesses faster than defenders can patch them, the asymmetry favours attackers. This is particularly acute for smaller ASPSPs and TPPs that lack dedicated security teams.

For the industry, this has regulatory implications. Both the FCA's open finance framework and the EU's PSR assume a certain baseline of API security. If AI-driven vulnerability discovery materially changes the threat landscape, the performance and security standards for dedicated interfaces may need revisiting sooner than regulators currently anticipate.

*Editorial note: This story connects AI capability advances to a concrete operational risk for open banking infrastructure — relevant as both the UK and EU expand the scope of mandated data-sharing APIs.*
