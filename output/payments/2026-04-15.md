**UK government opens £16 billion payments tender with variable recurring payments in scope**
*The Government Commercial Agency's new procurement framework signals that account-to-account payments, including variable recurring payments, are now a serious option for public sector collections.*

The UK's Government Commercial Agency has published a tender notice for its Payment Solutions 3 framework, covering an estimated £16 billion-plus in annual government payment flows. The scope explicitly references variable recurring payments alongside traditional card and bulk payment methods, marking the first time this open banking payment type has featured prominently in a central government procurement of this scale.

For the payments industry, the inclusion matters because government procurement frameworks tend to shape adoption patterns across hundreds of public bodies for years. If variable recurring payments secure a formal place in the framework, it would provide a reliable volume base that could accelerate infrastructure investment and standardisation. It also gives account-to-account payment providers a route into one of the UK's largest single sources of payment volume, previously dominated by card schemes and direct debit.

The tender arrives as the UK's open banking ecosystem matures beyond retail use cases. Variable recurring payments remain relatively early-stage in commercial deployment, and a government endorsement through procurement could shift the calculus for banks and payment service providers weighing investment in the capability.

**Industry maps FCA open finance roadmap onto mortgages, pensions and insurance**
*Sector-specific reactions to the FCA's open finance consultation reveal where firms see the most immediate commercial opportunity, with mortgage access and advice emerging as the priority use case.*

The wave of industry responses to the FCA's open finance vision, published April 14, has coalesced around a clear theme: mortgage lending is where open finance will likely have its first material impact beyond payments. Multiple mortgage industry bodies and advisory firms have highlighted the potential for automated income and expenditure verification to widen access for underserved borrowers, such as the self-employed, while reducing processing times and costs.

The FCA's consultation paper envisages extending open banking-style data sharing to savings, investments, pensions and insurance by 2030. Research cited by Open Banking Limited and EY puts the combined economic impact of open banking and open finance at £7.4 billion per year. But the industry reaction suggests firms are already triaging the roadmap, concentrating near-term planning on mortgage and credit use cases where the data infrastructure is closest to readiness and the regulatory pain points are most acute.

Insurance and pensions attracted less immediate attention, though the FCA's framing of pension dashboards as a complementary initiative suggests these will follow. For firms building open finance capabilities, the signal is that mortgage-adjacent products offer the clearest path to early revenues, while the broader vision remains a medium-term planning exercise.

**Swiss fintechs face extraterritorial reach of PSD3 and the new payment services regulation**
*A Mondaq analysis sets out how PSD3 and the accompanying payment services regulation will affect Swiss financial firms serving EU customers, despite Switzerland sitting outside the single market.*

The forthcoming PSD3 framework and its companion payment services regulation will reshape licensing requirements in ways that extend well beyond the EU's borders. A new analysis from Mondaq examines the practical implications for Swiss fintechs, which occupy an unusual position: outside the EU regulatory perimeter but heavily interconnected with EU payment flows and customers.

Key changes include the merger of e-money and payment institution licence categories into a single framework, which simplifies the EU regime but creates new compliance questions for Swiss firms operating under bilateral arrangements. The analysis also flags that PSD3's stronger customer authentication and liability provisions will apply to transactions touching EU consumers, regardless of where the payment service provider is domiciled.

For European payments professionals, the Swiss case is illustrative of a broader pattern. As PSD3 and the payment services regulation move towards finalisation, third-country firms across the EEA periphery, from the UK to Norway, will need to assess their exposure. The direction of travel is clear: the EU is extending its regulatory surface area, and proximity to the single market no longer offers the degree of optionality it once did.

**Visa, Stripe and Standard Chartered join Tempo blockchain as validator nodes**
*Three major payment firms becoming the first external validators on a stablecoin payments network marks a step-change in institutional engagement with blockchain-based settlement.*

Visa, Stripe and Zodia Custody (Standard Chartered's digital asset arm) have become the first external validators on the Tempo blockchain, a network designed for stablecoin-based payments. The move goes beyond the advisory or exploratory roles that large payment firms have typically taken in blockchain projects; running a validator node means active participation in transaction verification and network governance.

The significance lies in who is involved rather than what the technology does. Visa and Stripe are the two largest card-adjacent payment networks globally, and Standard Chartered brings institutional custody credibility. Their joint participation signals that stablecoin settlement infrastructure is crossing from experimentation to operational commitment, at least for specific corridors and use cases.

For European payments professionals, this is worth watching in the context of the EU's Markets in Crypto-Assets regulation and the European Central Bank's ongoing work on a digital euro. If stablecoin payment rails gain traction with firms of this scale, they become a competitive factor in cross-border settlement, an area where European infrastructure has historically relied on SWIFT and correspondent banking. The question is no longer whether institutional players will engage with blockchain-based payments, but which networks will attract critical mass.

*Editorial note: Visa, Stripe and Standard Chartered simultaneously becoming validators moves this beyond a vendor announcement into a signal about institutional positioning in blockchain settlement.*

**Financial data standards body launches initiative to address AI agents in data sharing**
*As AI agents begin to act on behalf of consumers in financial data requests, a standards body is moving to establish guardrails before the technology outpaces existing consent frameworks.*

A leading financial data standards body has launched a new initiative focused on the role of AI agents in open finance data sharing, according to a GlobeNewswire announcement. The initiative responds to a specific and growing concern: as AI-powered agents increasingly act autonomously on behalf of users, requesting and processing financial data through APIs, existing consent and authentication models designed for human-initiated interactions may not be fit for purpose.

The timing aligns with the FCA's open finance push and the broader expansion of permissioned data sharing across European markets. Current open banking consent flows assume a human user actively authorising each data request. AI agents that aggregate, analyse and act on financial data on a continuous basis challenge that model, raising questions about consent duration, scope and revocability.

For firms building open finance products, this is an early signal of a regulatory and standards gap that will need to be closed. The initiative is at a formative stage, but the underlying problem is real and will become more pressing as AI-driven financial tools proliferate. Firms that wait for formal regulation to catch up risk building products on consent architectures that will need to be reworked.

*Editorial note: The intersection of agentic AI and open finance consent frameworks is an emerging issue that will shape product design and regulatory expectations across the sector.*
