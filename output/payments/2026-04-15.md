## European Payments & Open Banking — April 15, 2026

**UK government launches £16 billion payments procurement with open banking in scope**
*The Government Commercial Agency's new payments tender explicitly includes variable recurring payments and account-to-account options, signalling that public sector procurement is now treating open banking rails as mainstream infrastructure.*

The UK's Government Commercial Agency has issued a tender notice for its Payment Solutions 3 framework, covering an estimated £16 billion-plus in public sector payment flows. The procurement specification goes beyond traditional card acquiring to include account-to-account payments and variable recurring payments, which allow flexible direct debits within pre-agreed limits.

This is notable less for the contract value than for what it normalises. Government procurement frameworks tend to lag commercial adoption by several years, so the explicit inclusion of open banking payment methods in a framework of this scale confirms that these rails have moved past the pilot phase in the UK. For payment service providers and open banking intermediaries, eligibility for public sector frameworks opens a large, stable revenue channel that has historically been dominated by card schemes and traditional acquiring.

*Editorial note: Public sector procurement rarely makes payments headlines, but a framework of this size formally incorporating variable recurring payments and account-to-account options is a concrete marker of open banking's maturation beyond retail use cases.*

**Financial data standards body launches initiative to govern AI agents in open finance**
*The Financial Data Exchange is pre-emptively setting standards for how autonomous AI agents interact with permissioned financial data APIs, before the technology outpaces the rules.*

The Financial Data Exchange (FDX), the US-Canadian standards body behind the FDX API specification used in open finance, has announced a new initiative focused on the role of AI agents in financial data sharing. The project aims to establish guardrails for how autonomous software agents access, interpret and act on user-permissioned financial data through existing API infrastructure.

The timing reflects a genuine gap. As large language model-powered agents begin to handle tasks such as account aggregation, product comparison and financial planning on behalf of consumers, existing consent and authentication frameworks were not designed for non-human intermediaries acting with delegated authority. The initiative will need to address questions around scope of consent, auditability and liability that current open banking and open finance standards leave unresolved.

For European observers, this is worth tracking closely. PSD3 and the FCA's open finance roadmap both assume human-initiated data sharing. If AI agents become a primary channel through which consumers interact with open finance infrastructure, the regulatory and technical assumptions underpinning both regimes will need updating. FDX setting standards early could influence how European frameworks evolve.

*Editorial note: This is an early-stage initiative but addresses a genuinely novel problem at the intersection of AI and open finance infrastructure, with direct implications for European standards development.*

**Swiss fintech sector assesses PSD3 implications despite sitting outside the EU**
*A detailed analysis of PSD3 and the Payment Services Regulation highlights how Switzerland's fintech industry must adapt to EU rules it had no part in shaping, a dynamic familiar to other third countries.*

A legal briefing published by Mondaq examines how PSD3 and the accompanying Payment Services Regulation will affect Swiss fintechs, many of which serve EU customers or depend on EU payment infrastructure. The analysis highlights PSD3's merger of the e-money and payment institution licence regimes, stronger customer authentication requirements, and expanded data access rights as areas where Swiss firms will need to adjust despite having no formal implementation obligation.

The Swiss case is instructive beyond its borders. PSD3's extraterritorial reach, through its effect on any firm accessing SEPA or serving EU consumers, means that third-country fintechs face compliance costs without regulatory voice. For UK firms navigating post-Brexit divergence, the parallel is direct: the FCA's open finance roadmap and PSD3 are heading in broadly similar directions, but differences in detail will create operational complexity for firms operating across both regimes.

The briefing also notes that PSD3's fraud liability provisions, which shift more responsibility onto payment service providers, could reshape risk models for cross-border payment firms that route through EU infrastructure.

*Editorial note: PSD3 coverage often focuses on EU-domiciled firms; this analysis of the Swiss perspective usefully illustrates the regulation's extraterritorial effects, relevant to any third-country payment provider.*

**Danske Bank exposes customer address data to payment recipients after system update**
*A configuration error during a routine system update caused Danske Bank to send protected customer address information to external payment recipients, raising questions about operational controls around data handling in payments.*

Danske Bank has confirmed that a human error during a planned system update led to customer address details being disclosed to outside payment recipients. The bank has not specified how many customers were affected or the duration of the exposure, but described the incident as resulting from a configuration mistake rather than a cyberattack.

The incident is minor in isolation but illustrative of a broader risk. As payment systems grow more complex, with richer data fields flowing through instant payment rails, ISO 20022 messaging and open banking APIs, the surface area for data leakage expands. Regulators across Europe have been tightening expectations around operational resilience, notably through DORA, which came into force in January. An incident of this kind, caused by routine maintenance rather than a sophisticated attack, is precisely the type of operational failure DORA's ICT risk management requirements are designed to prevent.

For banks preparing for DORA compliance reviews, this serves as a reminder that data protection failures in payments processing do not require a breach to create regulatory and reputational exposure.

*Editorial note: A contained incident, but it concretely illustrates operational resilience risks in payment systems at a moment when DORA enforcement is ramping up across Europe.*

**Visa, Stripe and Standard Chartered join Tempo blockchain as validator nodes**
*Three major payment infrastructure players becoming external validators on a stablecoin payments network marks a step-change in institutional engagement with blockchain-based payment rails.*

Visa, Stripe and Zodia Custody (Standard Chartered's digital asset custody arm) have become the first external validators on the Tempo blockchain, a network focused on stablecoin-based payments. The move goes beyond the advisory or investment roles that large payment firms have typically taken in blockchain projects: running validator nodes means these firms are directly participating in transaction processing and network governance.

The significance lies in the composition of the group. Visa operates the world's largest card network; Stripe processes payments for millions of online businesses; Standard Chartered is one of the most active banks in digital assets. Their simultaneous commitment to validating a stablecoin payments network suggests that institutional conviction around blockchain-based payment infrastructure has moved beyond experimentation.

For European payment firms, the implications are twofold. First, MiCA's stablecoin framework means EU-regulated entities now have a clear legal basis for participating in similar arrangements. Second, if stablecoin rails begin handling meaningful payment volumes with backing from firms of this scale, they become a competitive factor that incumbent processors and schemes will need to account for in their strategy.

*Editorial note: Institutional blockchain involvement in payments has been largely rhetorical until now; three firms of this calibre taking operational roles on a stablecoin network is a qualitative shift worth flagging.*
