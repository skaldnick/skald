## European Payments & Open Banking — April 16, 2026

**Euronet acquires Spanish payments firm PaynoPain to deepen European merchant reach**

*Euronet Worldwide's acquisition of PaynoPain extends its European merchant acquiring footprint into Spain's fast-growing digital payments market.*

Euronet, best known for its cross-border processing and ATM networks, has agreed to acquire PaynoPain, a Spanish fintech providing online payment solutions to merchants ranging from SMEs to large enterprises across e-commerce, hospitality, microfinance and marketplace verticals. The deal marks a deliberate push by Euronet into merchant acquiring in southern Europe, a segment where it has historically had limited presence.

The move fits a broader pattern of consolidation in European merchant payments, where scale and geographic coverage increasingly determine competitiveness. Spain's digital payments market has grown rapidly, driven by high smartphone penetration and strong consumer appetite for e-commerce. For Euronet, PaynoPain adds a digital-first acquiring platform to complement its existing processing and money transfer infrastructure.

The acquisition also signals that mid-tier European payment firms remain attractive targets as larger processors seek to fill geographic gaps ahead of regulatory changes such as PSD3 and the instant payments regulation, both of which will reshape competitive dynamics across the EU payments landscape.

**EU Commissioner ties open finance directly to Europe's AI competitiveness**

*Commissioner Albuquerque's Harvard speech frames open finance not just as a consumer empowerment tool but as essential infrastructure for competitive AI development in European financial services.*

Speaking at the 24th Annual Harvard Symposium, EU Commissioner Albuquerque made an explicit connection between open finance and AI strategy, arguing that open finance "is not only supporting innovation, it is a necessary condition for developing competitive and trustworthy AI in Europe's" financial sector. The framing matters: it elevates open finance from a consumer-rights initiative to a strategic pillar of Europe's broader competitiveness agenda.

This rhetorical positioning is significant for firms preparing for the Financial Data Access (FiDA) regulation. If the Commission views open finance as foundational to AI competitiveness, the political appetite for ambitious scope and tight timelines is likely to hold, even as industry lobbying pushes for longer implementation periods and narrower data-sharing obligations. It also suggests the Commission may be less sympathetic to arguments that data-sharing costs outweigh benefits.

The speech came days after the FCA published its own open finance roadmap with a 2030 target. While the UK and EU approaches differ in mechanism, both regulators are now framing open finance as a platform for broader innovation rather than a narrow extension of payment account access.

*Editorial note: A senior EU policymaker explicitly linking open finance to AI strategy signals how the Commission is likely to approach FiDA negotiations, which is directly actionable intelligence for firms scoping compliance and product strategies.*

**Trustly hires US tech executive as CTO amid push to scale pay-by-bank globally**

*The Swedish pay-by-bank firm's appointment of Randy Kern as CTO and Chantelle Zemba as CLO reflects its shift toward a data-driven, platform-scale business model.*

Trustly has appointed Randy Kern as chief technology officer and Chantelle Zemba as chief legal officer. Kern brings experience scaling global technology platforms, while Zemba's appointment addresses the growing regulatory complexity of operating across multiple open banking regimes. The company framed the CTO hire explicitly around the need for leadership in data-driven open banking payments.

The appointments are notable less for the individuals than for what they reveal about where pay-by-bank is headed. Trustly's stated emphasis on data — not just payment initiation — suggests the firm is positioning to compete on the intelligence layer above the transaction, such as credit risk signals, conversion optimisation and fraud scoring. This mirrors a broader shift across the pay-by-bank sector, where the margin opportunity increasingly lies in the data generated by account-to-account flows rather than the payment itself.

For incumbents in European payments, the signal is that pay-by-bank providers are building out capabilities that move them closer to the value-added services traditionally offered by card networks and processors.

*Editorial note: Senior hires at a major pay-by-bank player illuminate how the sector is evolving beyond simple payment initiation toward data-driven platform competition.*

**Danske Bank exposes customer addresses in payment system update error**

*A human error during a routine system update at Denmark's largest bank leaked protected customer address data to external payment recipients, raising questions about operational risk controls in payment infrastructure upgrades.*

Danske Bank confirmed that a planned system update inadvertently transmitted customer address information to outside parties receiving payments. The bank described the cause as human error rather than a cyberattack or systemic vulnerability. The scope of the breach — how many customers were affected and over what period — has not been fully detailed.

The incident is a reminder that operational risk in payments often manifests not through sophisticated attacks but through routine processes going wrong. As European banks undertake significant infrastructure upgrades to meet instant payments deadlines and prepare for PSD3, the frequency and complexity of system changes is increasing. Each update carries exposure, and the Danske case illustrates how even well-resourced institutions can fail at the process-control level.

For compliance and operations teams, the lesson is straightforward: the current wave of regulatory-driven system changes demands commensurate investment in change management, testing environments and data-handling safeguards, particularly where personal data flows through payment rails.

*Editorial note: Operational failures during payment system upgrades are an under-discussed risk as European banks face a heavy infrastructure change agenda. This incident gives it a concrete reference point.*

**Visa joins Tempo blockchain as first external validator alongside Stripe**

*Visa, Stripe and Zodia Custody have become the first external validators on the Tempo stablecoin payments blockchain, marking a step toward institutional participation in stablecoin-based settlement infrastructure.*

Visa, Stripe and Standard Chartered's digital custody arm Zodia have joined the Tempo blockchain as its first external validator nodes. Tempo positions itself as a stablecoin-based payments network. The involvement of three established financial infrastructure players as validators — not simply as investors or partners — represents a qualitative shift in how traditional payments firms are engaging with blockchain-based settlement.

The significance lies in the model, not the specific network. Validator participation means these firms are directly involved in transaction consensus and network governance, rather than building on top of a blockchain at arm's length. For Visa, this extends a pattern of hedging across payment rails: card networks, real-time account-to-account payments and now stablecoin settlement. For Stripe, which has been expanding its stablecoin capabilities since its Bridge acquisition, it deepens its infrastructure-level commitment.

Whether Tempo itself achieves meaningful transaction volumes is uncertain. But the willingness of major payments incumbents to operate nodes on stablecoin networks signals that institutional-grade stablecoin infrastructure is moving from experimentation to early operational deployment.

*Editorial note: The entry of Visa, Stripe and a Standard Chartered subsidiary as blockchain validators — not just commercial partners — marks a meaningful escalation in how traditional payments firms are engaging with stablecoin rails.*
