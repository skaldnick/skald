## European Payments & Open Banking — April 16, 2026

**Euronet acquires Spanish payments fintech PaynoPain**
*Euronet Worldwide's acquisition of PaynoPain strengthens its European merchant acquiring position, adding digital-first payment capabilities across e-commerce, hospitality and marketplace verticals.*

Euronet Worldwide has agreed to acquire PaynoPain, a Spanish fintech offering online payment solutions to businesses ranging from SMEs to large enterprises. The deal gives Euronet a direct foothold in Spain's digital commerce infrastructure across sectors such as e-commerce, hospitality, microfinance and marketplace platforms.

The acquisition fits a pattern of US-listed payment processors consolidating European merchant-facing fintechs to build out their continental acquiring capabilities. Euronet, better known for its EFT processing and money transfer networks, has been steadily broadening its merchant services division. PaynoPain's digital-first positioning and multi-vertical client base give it a ready-made distribution layer in one of Europe's faster-growing e-commerce markets.

For European acquirers and payment service providers, the deal is another signal that mid-market merchant payments businesses with strong vertical penetration are attractive targets, particularly in southern European markets where digital payment adoption is still accelerating.

*Editorial note: A concrete M&A transaction that illustrates ongoing consolidation in European merchant acquiring, relevant to competitive strategy across the payments industry.*

**EU Commissioner frames open finance as prerequisite for trustworthy AI in financial services**
*European Commissioner Albuquerque told the Harvard Symposium that open finance is a necessary condition for competitive, trustworthy AI in Europe's financial sector, tying the regulatory framework directly to the EU's AI ambitions.*

Speaking at the 24th Annual Harvard Symposium, EU Commissioner Albuquerque argued that open finance is not merely a competition measure but an essential foundation for developing AI applications in European financial services. The framing explicitly links the open finance regulatory agenda under PSD3 and the Financial Data Access (FiDA) framework to the EU's broader strategy on artificial intelligence.

This is a notable rhetorical shift. Open finance in Europe has typically been positioned around consumer empowerment and competition. By recasting it as infrastructure for AI, the Commissioner is building a new political case for the framework at a moment when legislative progress on FiDA has been slower than hoped. The argument runs that without standardised, consent-driven data access, European financial AI will be built on fragmented or proprietary datasets, leaving the sector at a disadvantage.

For firms planning around FiDA's timeline, the speech signals that the Commission sees open finance as strategically urgent rather than a second-order regulatory exercise. It may also foreshadow attempts to accelerate implementation or strengthen the framework's scope.

*Editorial note: A senior EU policymaker explicitly linking open finance to AI competitiveness is a meaningful development in the political narrative around FiDA and PSD3, with implications for how the framework is prioritised.*

**Trustly appoints CTO and CLO as it scales pay-by-bank infrastructure**
*The Swedish pay-by-bank provider has hired Randy Kern as CTO and Chantelle Zemba as chief legal officer, signalling investment in platform scalability and regulatory capacity as open banking payments grow more complex.*

Trustly has appointed Randy Kern as chief technology officer and Chantelle Zemba as chief legal officer. Kern brings experience scaling global platforms, while Zemba's appointment reflects the growing regulatory complexity facing payment initiation providers as open banking regimes evolve across multiple jurisdictions.

The hires are notable for what they reveal about where pay-by-bank is heading. Trustly's CEO framed the CTO appointment around the expectation that open banking payments will become "increasingly data driven", suggesting the company sees its future less as a pure payment initiation layer and more as a data-enriched commerce platform. The simultaneous investment in legal leadership points to the compliance burden of operating across regulatory regimes that are diverging in their approaches to open banking, from PSD3 in Europe to Section 1033 in the US.

For the pay-by-bank segment more broadly, the pattern of hiring for scale and regulatory sophistication, rather than market evangelism, marks a shift. The companies in this space are now building for operational complexity rather than proof of concept.

*Editorial note: Beyond a routine appointment story, this illustrates how pay-by-bank providers are maturing operationally as the sector moves from early adoption to infrastructure buildout.*

**Danske Bank exposes customer address data to payment recipients after system update error**
*A human error during a planned system update at Danske Bank caused protected customer address details to be shared with external payment recipients, raising questions about data governance in payment processing environments.*

Danske Bank has confirmed that a system update error led to customer address data being leaked to outside payment recipients. The bank attributed the breach to human error during a planned update, rather than a cyberattack or systemic vulnerability.

The incident is a reminder that data protection risk in payments often sits in operational processes rather than in sophisticated threat vectors. As payment systems grow more interconnected, with open banking and instant payments increasing the volume and velocity of data flows between institutions, the surface area for this type of operational leak expands. Under GDPR, even unintentional exposure of personal data to unintended recipients constitutes a breach, with potential regulatory consequences.

For banks and payment processors managing ongoing system modernisation, the Danske case underscores the importance of automated safeguards in deployment pipelines. Manual processes in environments handling personal data are an increasingly untenable risk.

*Editorial note: A concrete data governance failure at a major Nordic bank, relevant to the broader challenge of managing personal data across modernising payment systems.*

**Visa joins Tempo stablecoin blockchain as external validator alongside Stripe**
*Visa, Stripe and Zodia Custody have become the first external validators on the Tempo stablecoin payments blockchain, marking a step toward institutional participation in blockchain-based payment rails.*

Visa, Stripe and Zodia Custody (Standard Chartered's digital asset custody arm) have launched validator nodes on the Tempo blockchain, a network designed for stablecoin-based payments. The three are the first external validators to join the network, moving it from a closed system toward broader institutional participation.

The significance lies less in Tempo itself than in what the participation of Visa and Stripe signals. Both companies have been steadily building stablecoin capabilities: Visa through its various blockchain settlement pilots, Stripe through its acquisition of Bridge last year. Running validator nodes represents a deeper level of infrastructure commitment than simply supporting stablecoin transactions as a payment method.

For European payments professionals, the development is worth watching because stablecoin rails are increasingly being positioned as an alternative to traditional correspondent banking for cross-border settlement. If networks such as Tempo gain institutional credibility through participants of this calibre, they could begin to compete with established cross-border payment infrastructure sooner than many incumbents expect.

*Editorial note: Major payment network operators committing infrastructure to a stablecoin blockchain is a meaningful signal about the trajectory of alternative payment rails.*
