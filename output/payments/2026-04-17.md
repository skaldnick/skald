**Bancomat, EPI and SIBS complete cross-border QR payment interoperability proof of concept**

*Three of Europe's domestic payment schemes have demonstrated QR-code interoperability across borders, a practical step towards an account-to-account alternative to card networks at the point of sale.*

Bancomat (Italy), EPI and SIBS-MB WAY (Portugal) have completed a proof of concept enabling consumers to pay via QR code at merchants in another participating country, settling through instant payment rails. The trial is significant because it moves the European debate about payment sovereignty from policy papers to working demonstrations. If QR interoperability scales, it offers merchants a lower-cost acceptance channel and gives domestic schemes a viable cross-border proposition.

The timing matters. EPI's wero wallet is still building merchant acceptance, and the EU's instant payments regulation is pushing banks to ensure full SEPA instant reachability by late 2025. A working cross-border QR layer would give these efforts a consumer-facing surface that card schemes currently monopolise. The question now is whether participation extends beyond three schemes and whether settlement economics hold up at scale.

*Editorial note: This is the most consequential European payments story in today's feed — it signals tangible progress on a pan-European instant payment acceptance network that could reshape point-of-sale competition with card schemes.*

**Experian acquires Konfir to add payroll and employment verification**

*The credit bureau's purchase of UK-based Konfir consolidates open banking-derived income data, employer verification and credit scoring into a single provider, raising the bar for affordability assessment tooling.*

Experian has acquired Konfir, a verification platform that uses open banking connections, HMRC data and document processing to confirm employment and income for lending decisions. The acquisition brings payroll verification — an increasingly important signal for responsible lending — under Experian's roof alongside its existing credit data and banking insights capabilities.

For lenders, the deal simplifies a fragmented supplier landscape. Affordability checks currently require stitching together credit bureau data, open banking account information and separate income verification. Experian is betting it can offer all three in one integration. For open banking infrastructure providers, the deal is a reminder that the commercial value of account data is migrating towards firms that can combine it with other datasets rather than those that simply provide raw connectivity.

*Editorial note: This acquisition illustrates how open banking data is being absorbed into established credit infrastructure rather than remaining a standalone fintech proposition — a trend with strategic implications for the open banking ecosystem.*

**ADIB becomes first UAE bank licensed as an open finance provider**

*Abu Dhabi Islamic Bank has secured a third-party provider licence under the UAE Central Bank's AlTareq framework, marking the first bank-held TPP licence in the country and a milestone for open finance outside Europe.*

ADIB's licence allows it to operate as both an account information and payment initiation service provider under the UAE's open finance regime. The Central Bank of the UAE launched AlTareq to create a regulated framework for data sharing across banking, insurance and investment — broader in scope than PSD2's banking-only focus from the outset.

The significance for European observers is twofold. First, the UAE is implementing open finance with a scope that mirrors where Europe is heading under PSD3/PSR and the FCA's recently published open finance roadmap, but without the legacy of retrofitting an existing open banking framework. Second, the fact that a bank — not a fintech — is the first licensed TPP inverts the pattern seen in Europe, where banks were compelled to open APIs and third parties rushed to use them. ADIB is positioning itself on both sides of the data-sharing relationship, a strategy European banks have been slower to adopt.

*Editorial note: The UAE's open finance framework offers a useful counterpoint to European approaches, and a bank securing the first TPP licence there is a noteworthy strategic signal for European institutions considering their own open finance positioning.*

**Stockholm-based SolvaPay raises €2.4 million to build agentic payments infrastructure**

*A pre-seed round for an AI-native payments startup signals that venture capital is beginning to fund infrastructure purpose-built for autonomous agent-initiated transactions, not just agent-assisted workflows.*

SolvaPay, based in Stockholm, has raised €2.4 million to develop payment infrastructure designed for agentic commerce — scenarios in which AI agents initiate and complete transactions on behalf of consumers or businesses without manual intervention at each step. The round is modest, but the positioning is notable: SolvaPay is not adding AI features to an existing payments product but building rails specifically for machine-to-machine payment flows.

Agentic commerce sits at the intersection of two active European debates: how open banking payment initiation evolves beyond its current consumer-facing use cases, and how AI agents interact with regulated financial infrastructure. Worldline published a video on the topic this week, and the concept featured in recent EPC discussions about future SEPA scheme requirements. Whether autonomous agents become a material payment initiation channel depends on solving authentication, liability and consent challenges that current frameworks were not designed for. SolvaPay's fundraise is early-stage, but it reflects a bet that these problems are solvable and the market is forming now.

*Editorial note: Selected not for the funding amount — which is small — but because agentic payments infrastructure is an emerging theme that European payments professionals need to track, and this is one of the first dedicated funding rounds in the space.*

**Banks cannot solve UK fraud alone, argues former PSR executive**

*A former Payment Systems Regulator executive writing in The Banker argues that the current model of placing fraud liability primarily on banks is unsustainable without broader ecosystem accountability.*

The article, authored by Capgemini UK's managing director for financial crime advisory and a former PSR executive, contends that banks are absorbing fraud costs and operational burden that should be shared across telecoms, social media platforms and other enablers in the fraud chain. The argument is not new, but its source gives it weight: it comes from someone who helped shape the regulatory approach now being critiqued.

This matters in the context of the PSR's mandatory reimbursement regime for authorised push payment fraud, which took effect in 2024. Banks have repeatedly argued that reimbursement obligations without corresponding duties on upstream enablers create a moral hazard. The article appears to signal that this view is gaining traction beyond the banking lobby. Whether it influences the PSR's forthcoming review of the APP fraud reimbursement cap — expected later this year — remains to be seen, but the direction of the argument is clear: the current settlement is unstable.

*Editorial note: Fraud liability allocation is one of the most commercially significant policy debates in UK payments, and a former regulator adding their voice to the call for change is worth noting.*

*Sources: [Banks cannot save the UK financial system from fraud alone](https://www.thebanker.com/content/b19eacbc-2e24-4627-b9eb-986627e03bec) — The Banker*
