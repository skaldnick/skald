**Account-to-account payments near an inflection point, but merchant adoption lags**

*A Paypers overview of account-to-account payment adoption finds regulatory infrastructure outpacing commercial uptake, particularly at the point of sale in Europe.*

The Paypers has published an assessment of account-to-account payment adoption ahead of a third edition of its annual report, noting that while Europe's regulatory plumbing for instant payments and open banking is largely in place, merchant acceptance remains uneven. The piece frames the gap as one of commercial incentive rather than technical capability: rails exist, but checkout integration, consumer habit and refund handling continue to slow take-up.

The timing is relevant. With the EU's Instant Payments Regulation now bedding in and PSD3 discussions advancing, the question of whether account-to-account payments can move beyond bill pay and into mainstream retail is becoming more pressing. The overview highlights digital wallets as a potential bridge, acting as a consumer-facing layer on top of account-to-account rails, but notes this creates its own intermediation challenges.

For European payments strategists, the picture is familiar but the emphasis is shifting. The constraint is no longer whether instant account-to-account payments work — it is whether the commercial model around them can compete with cards at scale.

*Editorial note: This piece captures a structural tension at the heart of European payments strategy — the gap between regulatory readiness and commercial adoption of account-to-account payments — and is relevant to product and strategy teams across the ecosystem.*

**Bank of Thailand pushes for open finance framework to reach financially invisible citizens**

*Thailand's central bank is building a data-sharing framework aimed at extending credit access to digitally active consumers who remain invisible to traditional underwriting.*

The Bank of Thailand has called for an open finance framework to address what it describes as a structural gap: millions of citizens who are active digital consumers but lack the formal financial footprint needed to access credit. The central bank's argument is that fragmented data across banks, telecoms and e-commerce platforms leaves these consumers financially invisible, even where their transaction histories suggest creditworthiness.

The initiative adds Thailand to a growing list of Asian markets — alongside India's Account Aggregator framework and the Philippines' ongoing work — actively building open finance infrastructure. The approach echoes the inclusion-led rationale that shaped early open banking mandates in markets such as Mexico and Brazil, rather than the competition-focused model that drove PSD2 in Europe.

For European observers, the significance is comparative. As the FCA's open finance roadmap sets a path toward 2030 and PSD3 negotiations continue, the proliferation of open finance frameworks globally increases pressure on interoperability standards and cross-border data-sharing norms. Markets designing these systems now will shape whether open finance converges internationally or fragments.

*Editorial note: Selected because the spread of open finance frameworks beyond Europe is material to any firm with cross-border ambitions, and Thailand's inclusion-driven rationale offers a useful contrast to the European model.*

**CFPB faces pushback over accelerated job cuts as open banking rule hangs in balance**

*A federal employee union is challenging the pace of CFPB workforce reductions, adding further uncertainty to the US open banking rule's implementation.*

The National Treasury Employees Union has pushed back against what it calls an "artificial deadline" for CFPB job cuts, as the agency moves to reduce headcount and cancel office leases. Banking Dive reports that the reductions are proceeding under direction from Russell Vought, with the CFPB's operational capacity now a live question.

The workforce cuts matter for European payments firms because the CFPB's Section 1033 open banking rule — finalised in late 2024 — depends on the agency's ability to supervise compliance and manage the accreditation framework for data recipients. With staffing under pressure, the practical timeline for US open banking implementation becomes harder to predict, even if the rule itself remains on the books.

For European firms planning US market entry or transatlantic data-sharing strategies, the regulatory uncertainty is a material planning constraint. The contrast with the UK's structured FCA roadmap and the EU's legislative process around PSD3 is stark: the US has a rule but may lack the institutional capacity to enforce it.

*Editorial note: The CFPB's capacity directly affects when and how US open banking takes effect, which has strategic implications for any European payments or fintech firm with US exposure.*

**UK gambling affordability checks may accelerate open banking use case**

*The UK Gambling Commission's evolving approach to financial vulnerability checks is creating a concrete demand signal for open banking data in a regulated sector.*

The London Economic reports that the UK gambling industry's financial affordability checks are prompting operators to explore open banking as a verification mechanism. The UK Gambling Commission has previously identified open banking as a potential route for conducting these checks with less friction than traditional document-based approaches.

This is a practical test of open banking's utility beyond payments. If operators adopt open banking-based affordability checks at scale, it would represent one of the first significant data-sharing use cases in a regulated UK sector outside financial services proper. The FCA's open finance roadmap, published earlier this month, explicitly contemplates broadening data access beyond banking — gambling affordability checks could become an early proof point.

The use case also raises questions about consumer consent architecture and data minimisation. Operators need to verify affordability without accessing more financial data than necessary, which will test the granularity of open banking APIs and the adequacy of existing consent frameworks.

*Editorial note: This story illustrates how open banking is finding traction in adjacent regulated sectors, making it relevant to firms building data-sharing infrastructure and to policy professionals tracking open finance adoption.*

**Finexer positions open banking infrastructure for UK SaaS platforms ahead of VRP mandate**

*An FCA-authorised provider is targeting mid-market SaaS platforms as open banking clients, citing the 2027 variable recurring payments mandate and Making Tax Digital as demand drivers.*

Finexer, an open banking infrastructure provider, has announced a push to serve scaling UK SaaS platforms, arguing that regulatory requirements are creating a structural need for embedded open banking capabilities. The company points to the Making Tax Digital regime, now in effect, and the mandated rollout of variable recurring payments by 2027 as catalysts for adoption among platforms that previously treated open banking integration as optional.

The broader signal here is that variable recurring payments — long discussed as the next phase of UK open banking — are beginning to shape commercial planning even before the mandate takes full effect. If mid-market SaaS platforms start building variable recurring payment capability now, it suggests the 2027 deadline is being taken seriously as a hard constraint rather than an aspirational target.

The announcement is a vendor story, but the underlying dynamic is worth tracking: the intersection of tax digitisation and payment mandates is creating a specific, time-bound adoption driver for open banking in the UK's business software ecosystem.
