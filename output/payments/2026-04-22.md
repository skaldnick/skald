**UK Treasury confirms PSR abolition and hands FCA new open banking powers**

*HM Treasury will fold the Payment Systems Regulator into the FCA and give the combined body explicit authority over open banking payments, marking the most significant restructuring of UK payments oversight in a decade.*

The consultation response published on April 21 confirms the government's intention to abolish the PSR as a standalone entity and transfer its functions to the FCA. The move, first signalled last year, is now backed by a detailed policy response and draft legislative instruments. The FCA will inherit the PSR's existing remit over designated payment systems and gain new powers to regulate open banking, including authority to underpin commercial payment schemes built on open banking rails.

The package goes further than regulatory consolidation. HM Treasury simultaneously announced plans to bring stablecoins and tokenised deposits within the regulated payments perimeter, and flagged work on regulatory treatment of payments initiated by AI agents. The government is also exploring how to reduce administrative burdens on payment firms, an implicit acknowledgement that the current licensing regime has become a drag on competition.

For the industry, the practical question is transition. The PSR's approach to interchange, APP fraud reimbursement and scheme access will need to be absorbed into the FCA's supervisory model without creating gaps. The explicit grant of open banking powers to the FCA is arguably more consequential than the merger itself, giving the regulator a mandate it has previously lacked to shape how commercial open banking evolves beyond the CMA9 framework.

**FCA's Rusu sets out vision for open finance as foundation of UK fintech strategy**

*FCA chief data and innovation officer Jessica Rusu used a public speech to position open finance as central to the regulator's next phase of fintech support, citing 16 million open banking users as a base to build on.*

Speaking on April 21, Rusu outlined how the FCA sees open finance providing the infrastructure for a "more intelligent financial system", moving beyond payments data into broader financial product categories. The speech comes shortly after the FCA published its open finance roadmap on April 14 and sits alongside this week's HM Treasury announcements granting the FCA new open banking powers.

The speech also referenced the FCA's Scale-Up unit for solo-regulated firms and the second cohort of its AI live testing programme, which selected eight firms to explore AI applications under regulatory supervision. The framing is deliberate: the FCA is signalling that open finance, AI and the data-sharing powers granted under the Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 are being treated as a single policy programme rather than separate workstreams.

For firms building on open banking infrastructure, the message is that the regulatory runway is lengthening. The combination of statutory open finance powers under the DUAA, the FCA's new open banking mandate from Treasury and a regulator publicly advocating for the model creates a more favourable environment than firms have had since the original CMA order. The question remains whether rule-making will keep pace with the rhetoric.

*Editorial note: Rusu's speech connects several concurrent UK policy threads — the DUAA, FCA open finance roadmap and Treasury's payments package — and is significant as a regulatory signal of direction.*

**ION Treasury takes EU verification of payee live at scale, starting with Germany**

*ION Treasury has implemented verification of payee capabilities to meet the EU Instant Payments Regulation deadline, with Germany as the first market to go live.*

The EU's requirement for payment service providers to offer verification of payee on instant credit transfers is now operational, and ION Treasury's announcement illustrates how the compliance infrastructure is being deployed. Germany's early adoption reflects both its market size and the readiness of its payment service providers. ION says its solution is designed to scale across European markets as the mandate extends.

Verification of payee is the EU's primary technical measure to reduce misdirected payments and authorised push payment fraud. The UK's Confirmation of Payee scheme has been live since 2020, providing a reference point, but the EU implementation applies across a more fragmented banking landscape with greater variation in technical readiness. The practical challenge now shifts from building the capability to achieving consistent user experience across jurisdictions.

*Editorial note: The VoP rollout is a concrete milestone in the EU Instant Payments Regulation's implementation and signals the compliance phase is now underway across the bloc.*

**UK government publishes Smart Data 2035 strategy, positioning open finance as next chapter**

*The government's long-term data strategy treats open finance as the direct successor to open banking, setting out a cross-sector framework for regulated data sharing under the Data (Use and Access) Act.*

The Smart Data 2035 strategy, published alongside this week's broader payments and fintech package, maps out how the data-sharing model pioneered by open banking will extend into other financial products and eventually other sectors entirely. Open finance is presented as the most advanced near-term application, with HM Treasury and the FCA jointly responsible for defining scope and rules.

The strategy draws explicitly on the DUAA's provisions, which give regulators the power to mandate data-sharing interfaces in new sectors without primary legislation. For financial services firms, the implication is that the regulatory architecture for open finance is no longer speculative — it has a statutory basis, a named regulator and a published strategy document. The timeline for sector-specific rules remains to be defined, but the direction of travel is now formalised.

*Editorial note: This strategy document converts the open finance concept from policy aspiration to planned programme with statutory backing, which is a meaningful step for firms planning product and compliance roadmaps.*

**European APP fraud regulation gathers momentum as continent watches UK reimbursement model**

*Regulators across Europe are studying the UK's mandatory APP fraud reimbursement regime as a potential template, with growing pressure to adopt comparable protections on the continent.*

A FinTech Global analysis published on April 20 examines how European policymakers are responding to the UK's PSR-mandated reimbursement framework, which requires payment firms to compensate APP fraud victims. The piece notes that the approach has drawn "considerable interest" from European counterparts, though no EU-wide equivalent is yet in force.

The timing is notable. The EU's verification of payee mandate, now going live, addresses one component of the fraud problem — misdirected payments — but does not create a reimbursement obligation for social engineering fraud. As instant payments become the norm across the Single Euro Payments Area, the gap between preventive measures and victim compensation will become harder for regulators to ignore. The UK's experience, including the operational challenges and cost disputes that have accompanied its regime, will inform those decisions.

*Editorial note: This piece connects the EU's VoP rollout with the broader policy question of APP fraud liability, which is increasingly relevant as instant payments scale across Europe.*
