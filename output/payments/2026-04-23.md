**UK Treasury confirms PSR merger into FCA and expands open banking powers**

*HM Treasury has announced a sweeping consolidation of UK payments regulation, folding the Payment Systems Regulator into the FCA and granting it new authority over open banking and AI-driven payments.*

The package, unveiled during London Fintech Week, represents the most significant restructuring of UK payments oversight since the PSR was established in 2015. By absorbing the PSR into the FCA, the government aims to eliminate the fragmented supervisory landscape that the industry has long criticised, creating a single regulator with responsibility spanning traditional payment systems, open banking, stablecoins and emerging AI-based payment services.

The reforms also bring payments regulation formally within the FSMA framework for the first time, aligning it with the broader financial services rulebook. Stablecoins used for payments will be regulated alongside conventional instruments rather than through standalone crypto-specific rules. The FCA will gain explicit powers to oversee commercial open banking schemes, filling a gap that has persisted since the CMA's Open Banking Implementation Entity wound down.

For the industry, the practical question is pace: consolidating two regulators while simultaneously extending scope into stablecoins and AI payments is ambitious. Firms operating across multiple payment types should benefit from a single supervisory relationship, but the transition period will demand close attention to how the FCA absorbs the PSR's existing work on interchange, APP fraud reimbursement and access requirements.

**FCA publishes open finance roadmap with first scheme discussion paper due Q4 2026**

*The FCA has laid out a phased roadmap for extending data-sharing obligations beyond banking, targeting a first open finance scheme discussion paper by the end of this year.*

Building on the UK government's Smart Data Strategy, the FCA's plan draws explicitly on lessons from open banking — both its successes in standardisation and its well-documented struggles with commercial sustainability. The roadmap sets out objectives around competition, inclusive outcomes for consumers and SMEs, and economic resilience, but notably avoids prescribing which financial products will be brought into scope first.

The Q4 2026 discussion paper will be the first concrete test of the FCA's approach. The regulator has indicated it will focus initially on a single scheme before expanding, a sequenced strategy that contrasts with the EU's broader Financial Data Access (FiDA) proposal. For firms in insurance, pensions or investments, this timeline signals that regulatory expectations are moving from conceptual to operational within the next twelve months.

*Editorial note: The FCA roadmap moves open finance from aspiration to a concrete regulatory timeline, with direct relevance for firms planning data-sharing infrastructure and compliance.*

**Account-to-account payments face regulatory tailwinds but unresolved liability questions**

*A Paypers analysis maps how real-time payments regulation is accelerating account-to-account adoption across Europe while highlighting persistent gaps in payee verification and fraud liability.*

The piece examines how the Instant Payments Regulation, PSD3 proposals and national schemes such as Wero are collectively pushing account-to-account payments toward mainstream merchant acceptance. Instant payments as the default rail, combined with open banking initiation, creates the infrastructure for pay-by-bank to compete with cards at checkout. The regulatory direction is unambiguous.

The unresolved tension sits around liability. As account-to-account volumes grow, the absence of chargeback-equivalent protections and inconsistent payee verification standards across markets create friction that regulation has not yet addressed. PSD3's liability-shift provisions remain in negotiation, and the gap between the speed of adoption and the maturity of consumer protection frameworks is widening. For payment service providers and merchants evaluating account-to-account strategies, understanding where liability will land is now as important as the technical integration.

**Montenegro positions for digital euro pilot as it builds instant payments infrastructure**

*Montenegro has expressed interest in becoming a pilot jurisdiction for the digital euro while developing a TIPS-connected instant payments infrastructure aligned with the Eurosystem.*

The country's finance minister confirmed at a panel on the future of digital payments in Europe that Montenegro is building a clone of the ECB's TARGET Instant Payment Settlement platform, positioning the non-EU state to participate in euro-area payment infrastructure before formal accession. The government has also signalled interest in being considered for early digital euro implementation.

For a Western Balkans country outside the EU but using the euro de facto, this is a notable move. It tests how far Eurosystem payment infrastructure can extend beyond EU borders and could serve as a template for other accession candidates. The practical significance for the broader European payments landscape is limited in the near term, but the precedent — a non-member state integrating with TIPS — is worth watching.

*Editorial note: A small but strategically interesting story that illuminates the expanding geographic reach of euro-area instant payment infrastructure.*

**SCHUFA's 2025 results reveal finAPI sale as open banking exits reshape credit bureau strategies**

*German credit bureau SCHUFA reported revenue growth for 2025 but flagged a material EBIT impact from divesting its open banking subsidiary finAPI.*

SCHUFA's annual results show that excluding the proceeds from the finAPI sale, operating profit fell 11.2% to €61 million. The disposal of the open banking provider marks a strategic retrenchment: SCHUFA acquired finAPI to embed account data into credit decisioning, but the sale suggests the integration thesis did not deliver expected returns.

The move is part of a broader pattern across European credit and data firms reassessing their open banking positions. Where early-cycle acquisitions were driven by the promise of PSD2-enabled data access, some incumbents are now concluding that open banking infrastructure is better held by specialists or payments firms than by credit bureaux. For open banking providers, this is a reminder that the buyer landscape is shifting: strategic acquirers from the first wave may become sellers, while payments processors and platforms with direct distribution become more natural owners.

*Editorial note: A specific corporate result that illuminates a wider trend — the unwinding of early open banking M&A by incumbents whose integration rationale has not held up.*
