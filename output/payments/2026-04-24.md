**UK government confirms FCA will absorb PSR and gain open banking powers**

*The UK Treasury has laid out a comprehensive payments reform package that folds the Payment Systems Regulator into the FCA and gives the combined body explicit authority over open banking's commercial future.*

The package, announced by the Economic Secretary, represents the most significant structural change to UK payments oversight in a decade. The PSR–FCA merger, long discussed, is now confirmed policy, consolidating supervision of payment systems, open banking and digital assets under one roof. The FCA will gain new powers to regulate open banking payments within commercial schemes, a move intended to resolve the long-running governance gap that has hampered the development of viable commercial models since the CMA's original open banking remedies.

Alongside the regulatory consolidation, the government confirmed it will bring stablecoins within the payment services regulatory perimeter, aligning with broader efforts to position the UK as a digital assets hub. Janine Hirt, CEO of Innovate Finance, highlighted the UK's opportunity to lead in open banking, digital assets and AI-driven finance, but the practical significance lies in the structural clarity: a single regulator with a mandate that spans traditional payments, open banking and digital assets.

For the industry, the immediate question is implementation timeline. The FCA will consult on its new open banking powers, but firms building on open banking infrastructure now have a clearer sense of where regulatory authority will sit.

*Editorial note: This is the biggest UK payments governance story in years, directly affecting how open banking is regulated and commercialised.*

**EU Council publishes progress note on PSD3, signalling trilogue positioning**

*The Council of the EU has issued an 'I' Item Note on PSD3, marking a formal step in member states' alignment ahead of trilogue negotiations with the European Parliament.*

Published on April 23 and dated April 17, the note reflects the Council's current position on the draft Payment Services Directive. 'I' Item Notes are informational records that capture the state of play without requiring ministerial discussion, but their publication signals that the Council presidency considers member state positions sufficiently developed to warrant formal documentation.

The timing matters. With the European Parliament having advanced its own position, the Council note is a procedural prerequisite for structured trilogue talks. For firms planning against PSD3 timelines, the note suggests negotiations are progressing, though the directive's final shape remains subject to the usual compromises between Council and Parliament positions on contentious issues such as liability frameworks, strong customer authentication scope and open finance data-sharing obligations.

*Editorial note: A procedural step, but a meaningful one for anyone tracking PSD3's legislative timeline and likely implementation date.*

**Mule network exploiting European instant payments exposes fintech onboarding gaps**

*A large-scale mule account network operating across French neobanks and fintechs such as Revolut, Wise and N26 has been identified laundering funds through instant payment rails, raising fresh questions about onboarding controls in the instant payments era.*

The fraud operation follows a three-phase model: accounts are opened at scale using synthetic or compromised identities, funds from upstream fraud are routed through them via instant payments, and the proceeds are dispersed before detection. The speed of instant settlement, which is the point of the infrastructure, is also what makes it attractive for laundering. Once funds move, they are effectively irrevocable.

The case is not the first of its kind, but its scale and cross-platform nature underscore a tension that regulators and the industry have been slow to resolve. The EU's instant payments regulation, which mandates SEPA instant credit transfer availability, has accelerated adoption without equivalently tightening the onboarding and transaction-monitoring standards that need to keep pace. For neobanks operating at volume with lightweight KYC processes, the economics of fraud prevention are being tested. The European Banking Authority's forthcoming guidance on fraud risk in instant payments will be closely watched.

*Editorial note: Instant payments fraud is a systemic concern for the European market as SEPA instant becomes mandatory; this case is a concrete illustration of the risks.*

**FCA open finance roadmap sets 2030 horizon for data-sharing beyond payments**

*The FCA has published an open finance roadmap targeting 2030 for extending regulated data-sharing to savings, investments, pensions and insurance, building on the existing open banking framework.*

The roadmap positions open finance as part of the UK's broader Smart Data programme, with the FCA envisaging a phased expansion that begins with use cases where consumer benefit is clearest, such as switching and financial advice. Hogan Lovells' analysis notes that while open banking already enables sharing of payments data, open finance would bring investment and pension data into scope, requiring new consent frameworks and technical standards.

The practical challenge is incentive design. Open banking's first decade demonstrated that mandating data access is necessary but not sufficient; viable commercial models require both regulatory clarity and willingness from data holders to participate constructively. The FCA's new powers over open banking, confirmed in this week's Treasury announcement, may give it more leverage to shape commercial outcomes in open finance than the CMA had in open banking's early years. Whether insurers and asset managers respond differently from banks remains to be seen.

*Editorial note: The roadmap gives the first concrete timeline for open finance in the UK, directly relevant to firms planning product strategies beyond payments data.*
