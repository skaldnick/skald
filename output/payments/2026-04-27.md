**ECB commits digital euro to European standards, sidelining global card schemes**

*The ECB has signed agreements with three European standard-setting bodies to build the digital euro's technical infrastructure, a deliberate move to reduce dependence on Visa and Mastercard.*

The ECB announced on April 24 that it has signed cooperation agreements with the European Payments Council, the Berlin Group and CEN (the European Committee for Standardisation) to develop the technical standards underpinning the digital euro. The agreements cover messaging formats, API specifications and security protocols. The Berlin Group's NextGenPSD2 framework, which already underpins roughly 80% of European open banking connectivity, will form the basis for account-linked payment interactions.

The strategic signal is clear: the digital euro's rails will be European-owned and European-governed. By anchoring the digital euro to existing open banking infrastructure rather than the card scheme networks that dominate point-of-sale and online payments today, the ECB is building on standards the market already knows. For banks and payment service providers, this means digital euro integration should align with capabilities they have already built for PSD2 compliance.

The practical implications will depend on how prescriptive the resulting specifications are and how quickly they crystallise. The preparation phase is ongoing, with no confirmed launch date. But for firms planning their digital euro readiness, the standards question, one of the larger unknowns, is now materially clearer.

**UK government prepares financial services reform bill merging PSR into FCA**

*A proposed UK financial services reform bill would fold the Payment Systems Regulator into the Financial Conduct Authority, the most significant restructuring of UK payments oversight since the PSR's creation in 2015.*

The UK government is set to introduce a Financial Services Reform Bill that would merge the PSR with the FCA. The move follows a period of sustained debate about regulatory overlap and the burden of dual supervision on payment firms. The bill would also revise the remit of the Financial Ombudsman Service.

For payments firms operating in the UK, the merger would consolidate two sets of regulatory expectations, reporting lines and enforcement approaches into one. The PSR has carved out a distinct role on interchange fee monitoring, access to payment systems and APP fraud reimbursement. How those functions are absorbed into the FCA's broader mandate, and whether they retain the same priority, will matter greatly. Firms currently managing separate PSR and FCA engagement strategies should begin considering how a unified regime might shift regulatory priorities and supervisory culture.

*Editorial note: A structural change to UK payments regulation with direct operational implications for any firm the PSR currently oversees.*

**iFAST Global Bank partners with Ozone API to adopt Open Banking 4.0**

*UK-based iFAST Global Bank has selected Ozone API as its open banking technology provider, aligning with the latest iteration of the UK's Open Banking Standard.*

iFAST Global Bank, the UK digital banking arm of Singapore-headquartered iFAST Corporation, has announced a partnership with Ozone API to implement Open Banking 4.0 capabilities. The move positions iFAST to meet evolving UK open banking requirements and signals the bank's intention to expand its role in the open finance ecosystem.

The partnership is a vendor selection rather than a market-shifting event, but it is notable as an indicator of where adoption of the Open Banking 4.0 standard stands. Ozone API, which provides conformance-tested open banking platforms, has been building a client base among smaller banks and newer market entrants that lack the internal capacity to build compliant APIs from scratch. For the broader market, each adoption of the 4.0 standard adds to the critical mass needed to make open finance services commercially viable beyond the largest institutions.
