**UK financial services bill to merge PSR into FCA**

*The UK government will use its forthcoming financial services bill to fold the Payment Systems Regulator into the Financial Conduct Authority, the most significant restructuring of UK payments oversight in a decade.*

The bill, expected to feature in the King's Speech, will consolidate payments regulation under the FCA and reform the Financial Ombudsman Service, according to reporting by the Financial Times. The move follows sustained industry criticism of jurisdictional overlap between the PSR and FCA, particularly around fraud liability, APP scam reimbursement and open banking supervision. The government has framed it as part of a broader effort to cut regulatory red tape for financial services.

For payments firms, the practical question is how the FCA will absorb the PSR's remit without losing specialist focus. The PSR has driven consequential interventions in interchange, access to payment systems and fraud reimbursement. How those workstreams are prioritised within a larger regulator — and whether the FCA gains new statutory powers over open banking as part of the same legislation — will matter more than the organisational chart.

The timing also coincides with the departure of senior PSR staff. Charity Times reported that the PSR's chief operating officer is moving to a foundation role, a sign that the merger's gravitational pull is already affecting the regulator's bench.

*Editorial note: The PSR-FCA merger is the most consequential UK payments regulatory development of the year, with direct implications for how open banking, fraud policy and payment system access are governed.*

**FCA outlines next phase for open finance and fintech scale-up support**

*The FCA has published an open finance roadmap and opened its Scale Up Unit to applications, signalling a shift from sandbox experimentation to supervised commercialisation.*

An A&O Shearman analysis of a recent FCA speech and the regulator's 2025 innovation insights report highlights two connected moves. First, the FCA has laid out a roadmap for open finance, extending data-sharing obligations beyond payments accounts. Second, the Scale Up Unit — designed to help firms that have outgrown the regulatory sandbox navigate full authorisation — is now accepting firms.

The open finance roadmap matters because it sets the direction for how the FCA will exercise whatever new powers it receives under the forthcoming financial services bill. If the PSR merger gives the FCA explicit authority over open banking, the roadmap becomes the operational framework. The Scale Up Unit addresses a long-standing complaint from fintechs: that regulatory support drops off precisely when firms need it most, at the transition from pilot to commercial deployment.

*Editorial note: With the PSR merger pending, the FCA's open finance roadmap and expanded innovation support indicate how the regulator intends to use its enlarged mandate. Relevant context for the lead story.*

**ETPPA launches German chapter to push pay-by-bank adoption**

*Token.io and co-leads have established a German chapter of the European Third Party Providers Association, marking a structured industry effort to build account-to-account payment acceptance in Europe's largest economy.*

The new chapter, co-led by Token.io's Gideon Fourie, aims to promote pay-by-bank and open finance across the German market. Germany has been a persistent laggard in open banking adoption relative to the UK, the Nordics and the Netherlands, despite its market size. Strong incumbency of cash and girocard, combined with cautious bank engagement, has limited third-party provider traction.

The ETPPA chapter matters because Germany is a necessary market for any pan-European account-to-account payments proposition. Without meaningful German merchant acceptance, pay-by-bank remains a collection of national success stories rather than a continental payment method. The chapter structure suggests the industry recognises that German adoption requires dedicated advocacy and local coordination, not just regulatory mandate.

*Editorial note: Germany's account-to-account payments gap is one of the largest unresolved questions in European open banking. A dedicated trade body chapter is a practical signal of where the industry sees the next battleground.*

**European local payment schemes eye cross-border expansion**

*An expert analysis in The Paypers examines how national instant payment schemes are attempting to extend beyond domestic markets, a trend that will test both technical interoperability and regulatory readiness.*

The piece explores the strategic logic driving local payment champions — schemes such as iDEAL, Swish and Bizum — as they consider cross-border reach. Domestic success has given these schemes scale and consumer trust, but expansion exposes structural challenges: differing regulatory regimes, the absence of unified addressing schemes and competition from card networks that already operate seamlessly across borders.

The cross-border question is sharpened by the EU's instant payments regulation, which mandates euro instant payment reachability but does not resolve how domestic overlay services connect to one another. Whether interoperability emerges through bilateral links, a pan-European scheme such as EPI/Wero, or market consolidation remains an open question with significant commercial stakes for processors and banks alike.

*Editorial note: The tension between national payment scheme success and cross-border ambition is one of the defining strategic questions in European payments. This analysis offers a useful framework for professionals tracking scheme strategy.*

**Digital euro positioned as strategic sovereignty tool alongside instant payments**

*ECB and EU officials are framing the digital euro as part of a broader payments sovereignty package that includes instant payments and wholesale settlement infrastructure.*

Reporting from Cyprus Mail captures a senior official's characterisation of the digital euro not as a standalone project but as one component of a strategy encompassing instant payments and wholesale digital settlement. This framing is deliberate: it ties the digital euro's political justification to concerns about European dependence on non-European payment networks, a message that has gained traction amid geopolitical uncertainty.

The sovereignty narrative serves a practical purpose. By coupling the digital euro with instant payments — which already have legislative backing and implementation deadlines — officials make the digital euro appear less speculative and more like a natural extension of existing infrastructure. For payments firms, the implication is that digital euro design choices will be influenced by instant payment rails architecture, and that firms already investing in instant payment compliance are building toward a broader settlement ecosystem.

*Editorial note: The explicit linkage between digital euro and instant payments strategy reflects how policymakers are building the political case for the digital euro — relevant for any firm planning around euro-area payment infrastructure.*
