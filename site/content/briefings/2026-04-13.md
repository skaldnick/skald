**GoCardless hits first profitable quarter while cutting 90 roles**
*The open banking payments firm's path to profitability — doubling volumes to £79.2bn while trimming headcount — tests whether account-to-account payment providers can sustain margins at scale.*

GoCardless reported 22% revenue growth in FY25 and its first quarter of positive adjusted EBITDA, a milestone for a company that has been a bellwether for open banking-powered payments in Europe. Payment volumes doubled to £79.2bn, boosted significantly by its earlier acquisition of open banking firm Nuapay, which brought instant bank payment capabilities alongside its core direct debit business.

The profitability milestone comes with a restructuring: roughly 90 roles are being cut. The combination of strong volume growth with headcount reduction suggests the company is shifting from a growth-at-all-costs phase to one focused on unit economics — a transition the broader open banking payments sector is watching closely. The question for account-to-account payment providers has always been whether thin per-transaction margins can support a viable standalone business. GoCardless's results offer early, qualified evidence that they can, at least for firms that have reached sufficient scale.

CEO Hiroki Takeuchi positioned the results as validation of the company's model. For the sector, the more interesting signal is structural: open banking payment volumes are now large enough to generate real operating leverage, but the path there requires the kind of consolidation (Nuapay) and cost discipline (headcount cuts) that distinguishes infrastructure businesses from high-growth fintechs.

*Editorial note: GoCardless is one of the most significant independent open banking payment providers in Europe; its first profitable quarter is a meaningful data point on the commercial viability of A2A payments at scale.*

**Wise to shift primary listing from London to New York next month**
*The move, following strong Q4 results, is the latest departure from the London Stock Exchange and raises questions about Europe's ability to retain its largest payments fintechs.*

Wise has confirmed it will transfer its primary listing to the United States next month, ending its run as one of the London Stock Exchange's highest-profile fintech listings. The announcement accompanied Q4 results showing continued strong growth in volumes, customers and income.

The relisting follows a pattern now familiar in European fintech. London's public markets have struggled to deliver the valuations and analyst coverage available in New York, particularly for high-growth payments businesses. Wise joins a growing list of European-founded fintechs — including companies like Flutter Entertainment and CRH before it — that have concluded the US offers a more favourable capital markets environment. For European payments specifically, the departure of a company that processes tens of billions in cross-border flows is symbolically significant, even if its operational footprint in Europe will remain unchanged.

The practical implications are limited: Wise's regulatory authorisations, product availability and European customer base are unaffected by where its shares trade. But the listing shift underlines a structural challenge for European capital markets that policymakers — including those behind the UK's Edinburgh Reforms and the EU's Capital Markets Union — have so far failed to resolve.

*Editorial note: Wise's delisting from London is a significant event for the European payments industry, reflecting ongoing capital markets competitiveness challenges that directly affect how the sector funds and scales.*

**Federal Reserve consults on extending FedNow to cross-border payments**
*A proposal to enable FedNow participants to make international transfers could reshape the competitive landscape for cross-border instant payments — with direct implications for European corridors.*

The US Federal Reserve has opened a consultation on enabling cross-border payments through FedNow, its domestic instant payment system. The proposal would allow FedNow participants to route international transfers, a significant expansion of scope for a system that launched in 2023 as a purely domestic rail.

For European payments, this matters on two fronts. First, it signals the Fed's intent to compete more directly with established correspondent banking networks and with newer entrants — including fintechs and messaging-layer solutions — that have built businesses on the inefficiency of cross-border payments. Second, any interoperability between FedNow and European instant payment infrastructure (whether via bilateral links or intermediary models) would create a new pathway for euro-dollar flows that bypasses traditional rails. The EU's own instant payments regulation, which mandates SEPA instant credit transfer availability, provides the European-side infrastructure that would be a natural counterparty.

The consultation is at an early stage, and design details — particularly around FX settlement, sanctions screening and interoperability protocols — will determine whether this becomes a practical alternative or remains aspirational. But the direction of travel is clear: domestic instant payment systems on both sides of the Atlantic are being positioned as the foundation for cross-border connectivity, not just national utilities.

*Editorial note: FedNow's potential cross-border extension directly affects European payment processors and banks handling transatlantic flows, and advances the broader global trend of linking domestic instant payment systems.*

**EBA moves to harmonise SEPA reporting across member states**
*The decision addresses a long-standing inconsistency in how national regulators report under the SEPA Regulation, a necessary step as the instant payments mandate increases supervisory complexity.*

The European Banking Authority has published a decision harmonising the reporting frameworks used by national competent authorities under the SEPA Regulation. The move standardises what has until now been a fragmented patchwork of national approaches to data collection and supervisory reporting.

This is less dramatic than new regulation but arguably more consequential for day-to-day compliance. Inconsistent national reporting has been a persistent operational burden for payment service providers operating across multiple SEPA jurisdictions. Each market's regulator has historically defined its own data requirements, formats and timelines, creating duplicative work and making pan-European supervisory oversight more difficult. As the SEPA instant credit transfer mandate drives higher volumes and broader participation, the case for consistent reporting becomes harder to ignore.

The EBA's decision sets a common framework but leaves implementation to national authorities. The practical impact will depend on how quickly NCAs align their processes — and whether the harmonisation extends to the granularity of data requested, not just the reporting structure.

*Editorial note: Reporting harmonisation is an operational detail that directly affects compliance costs for any PSP operating across SEPA, and is a necessary piece of infrastructure as the instant payments mandate takes effect.*
